Ontology Specification Draft

Abstract

The Wearable PGHD-PROVO Ontology extends the PGHD-PROVO Ontology to specifically model Patient-Generated Health Data (PGHD) collected from wearable devices, such as fitness trackers and smartwatches. This ontology provides a structured framework for representing physical activity, sleep patterns, and vital signs (e.g., heart rate, blood pressure) alongside their provenance (who collected the data, when, and how). It integrates vendor-specific data properties (e.g., Fitbit, Withings) to ensure interoperability across different wearable ecosystems while maintaining semantic consistency. Key features include: Activity tracking (steps, calories, MET values) Sleep monitoring (sleep stages, efficiency, latency) Vital sign recording (heart rate, blood pressure, breathing rate) Provenance metadata (data source, collection time, responsible agents) This ontology supports personalized healthcare, remote patient monitoring, and clinical research by enabling standardized representation and sharing of wearable-derived health data.

Introduction back to ToC

Wearable devices have revolutionized healthcare by enabling continuous, real-time monitoring of patient health outside clinical settings. However, the lack of a standardized semantic model for wearable-generated data poses challenges for data integration, interpretation, and trustworthiness. The Wearable PGHD-PROVO Ontology addresses these challenges by: Extending PGHD-PROVO to include specialized classes for: DailyActivity: Tracks physical activity (steps, calories, intensity levels). Sleep: Captures sleep stages (REM, deep, light), duration, and efficiency. VitalSign: Records physiological metrics (heart rate, blood pressure). Harmonizing vendor-specific properties: Maps equivalent terms from Fitbit, Withings, and other vendors (e.g., sleepDuration vs. total_sleep_time). Uses propertySource annotations to indicate data origins. Enhancing provenance tracking: Links data to wearable devices (e.g., FitbitWearable), patients, and collection contexts. Supports data validation by documenting measurement protocols and device ratings. Use Cases Remote patient monitoring: Clinicians assess patient activity/sleep trends. Research: Aggregate wearable data across studies while preserving metadata. Interoperability: Convert vendor-specific datasets into a unified RDF format.
